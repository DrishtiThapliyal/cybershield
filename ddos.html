🌐 Distributed Denial-of-Service (DDoS)

🏷 Common Attacks 🟡 Intermediate ⏱ 2–3 min read

What is a DDoS Attack?

A Distributed Denial-of-Service (DDoS) attack is a cyber attack in which multiple compromised computers or devices flood a server, website, or network with massive amounts of traffic, making it slow or completely unavailable to legitimate users.

How Does It Work?

  1. Attackers infect thousands of devices with malware to create a botnet.
  2. The botnet receives commands from the attacker.
  3. All infected devices send huge amounts of requests to the target simultaneously.
  4. The server becomes overloaded and stops responding to genuine users.

Real-Life Example

An online shopping website experiences millions of fake requests from infected devices across the world during a sale. The website crashes, preventing customers from accessing its services until the attack is mitigated.

Warning Signs

  • Website becomes unusually slow.
  • Frequent server crashes or downtime.
  • Large spikes in network traffic.
  • Users cannot access online services.
  • High server resource usage.
  • Multiple requests from unknown IP addresses.

How to Stay Safe

  • Use DDoS protection services.
  • Implement firewalls and intrusion detection systems.
  • Monitor network traffic regularly.
  • Keep servers updated with security patches.
  • Use load balancing to distribute traffic.
  • Develop an incident response plan.

What to Do If You Are a Victim

  • Inform your hosting provider immediately.
  • Enable DDoS mitigation services.
  • Block malicious IP addresses if possible.
  • Monitor traffic continuously.
  • Inform users about temporary service disruption.
  • Review logs and strengthen security after recovery.

💡 Quick Tips

  • DDoS attacks target service availability rather than stealing data.
  • Cloud-based DDoS protection can reduce the impact of attacks.
  • Continuous monitoring helps detect attacks early.

🤔 Did You Know?

Some of the largest DDoS attacks have generated traffic exceeding several terabits per second, enough to disrupt major online platforms and services around the world.
