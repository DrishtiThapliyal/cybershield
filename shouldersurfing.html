👀 Shoulder Surfing

🏷 Social Engineering 🟢 Beginner ⏱ 2–3 min read

What is Shoulder Surfing?

Shoulder Surfing is a social engineering technique where an attacker secretly observes a person's screen, keyboard, documents, or activities to steal sensitive information such as passwords, PINs, banking details, or confidential data without the victim's knowledge.

How Does It Work?

  1. The attacker positions themselves near the victim.
  2. The victim enters sensitive information such as passwords or PINs.
  3. The attacker observes or records the information.
  4. The stolen information is later used for fraud or unauthorized access.

Real-Life Example

A person enters their ATM PIN while someone standing nearby secretly watches the keypad. The attacker later uses the observed PIN to access the victim's bank account.

Warning Signs

  • People standing unusually close while you enter passwords or PINs.
  • Someone repeatedly looking at your screen or keyboard.
  • Suspicious use of mobile phones or cameras nearby.
  • Crowded places where screens are easily visible.
  • Someone trying to distract you while entering confidential information.
  • Unknown individuals watching your activities closely.

How to Stay Safe

  • Cover the keypad while entering passwords or PINs.
  • Use privacy screen protectors on laptops and phones.
  • Stay aware of your surroundings.
  • Avoid accessing sensitive accounts in crowded places.
  • Lock your device when not in use.
  • Position your screen away from public view.

What to Do If You Are a Victim

  • Change exposed passwords or PINs immediately.
  • Monitor accounts for suspicious activity.
  • Contact your bank if financial information was exposed.
  • Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA).
  • Review your account security settings.
  • Report suspicious behaviour if necessary.

💡 Quick Tips

  • Always shield your screen and keypad.
  • Be extra cautious in airports, cafes, and public transport.
  • Lock your devices whenever you step away.

🤔 Did You Know?

Shoulder surfing requires no hacking skills or malware. Many successful attacks happen simply because victims are unaware that someone is watching them.
