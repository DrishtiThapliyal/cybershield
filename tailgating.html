🚪 Tailgating

🏷 Social Engineering 🟡 Intermediate ⏱ 2–3 min read

What is Tailgating?

Tailgating is a physical social engineering attack where an unauthorized person gains access to a restricted area by following an authorized person into the premises. Instead of hacking technology, attackers exploit human courtesy and trust.

How Does It Work?

  1. The attacker waits near a secured entrance.
  2. An authorized employee uses their access card to enter.
  3. The attacker follows closely behind without using credentials.
  4. Once inside, they may access sensitive systems or confidential information.

Real-Life Example

An employee enters an office building using their access card. A person carrying boxes asks them to hold the door open. Wanting to be helpful, the employee allows them in without verifying their identity, giving the attacker unauthorized access.

Warning Signs

  • People entering without an access card.
  • Unknown individuals asking someone to hold the door.
  • Visitors wandering in restricted areas.
  • Someone repeatedly claiming they forgot their access card.
  • Individuals avoiding security checkpoints.
  • People refusing to identify themselves.

How to Stay Safe

  • Ensure everyone uses their own access credentials.
  • Politely verify unfamiliar individuals.
  • Check visitor identification.
  • Follow organizational security policies.
  • Report suspicious behaviour immediately.
  • Never allow unknown people into secure areas.

What to Do If You Are a Victim

  • Report the incident to security personnel immediately.
  • Identify areas the unauthorized person accessed.
  • Review CCTV footage if available.
  • Change access credentials if necessary.
  • Follow your organization's incident response procedures.
  • Monitor for suspicious activity afterwards.

💡 Quick Tips

  • Being polite should never compromise security.
  • Always verify identities before granting access.
  • If unsure, contact security personnel.

🤔 Did You Know?

Many organizations conduct physical security awareness training because human behaviour is often the weakest link in access control systems.
