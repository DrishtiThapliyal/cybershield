🎭 Pretexting

🏷 Social Engineering 🟡 Intermediate ⏱ 2–3 min read

What is Pretexting?

Pretexting is a social engineering attack in which an attacker creates a fake identity or believable story to gain a victim's trust and obtain sensitive information, access, or assistance. The attacker carefully plans the interaction to appear legitimate.

How Does It Work?

  1. The attacker researches the target.
  2. A convincing fake identity or story is created.
  3. The attacker contacts the victim pretending to be someone trustworthy.
  4. The victim is persuaded to reveal confidential information or perform certain actions.

Real-Life Example

An attacker calls an employee pretending to be from the company's IT department. They claim there is a technical issue and ask for the employee's login credentials to "fix the problem." Once shared, the attacker gains unauthorized access.

Warning Signs

  • Requests for sensitive information without verification.
  • Unexpected contact from someone claiming authority.
  • Pressure to respond quickly.
  • Stories that are difficult to verify.
  • Requests that bypass normal procedures.
  • Someone becoming overly friendly to gain trust.

How to Stay Safe

  • Verify the identity of anyone requesting confidential information.
  • Follow official procedures before sharing data.
  • Be cautious of unexpected requests.
  • Never share passwords or sensitive information without authorization.
  • Contact the organization through official channels to verify requests.
  • Report suspicious interactions immediately.

What to Do If You Are a Victim

  • Inform the concerned organization immediately.
  • Change compromised passwords.
  • Monitor your accounts for unusual activity.
  • Report the incident to security personnel.
  • Review what information was shared.
  • Secure affected accounts and systems.

💡 Quick Tips

  • Trust should always be verified.
  • Never bypass security procedures.
  • Confirm identities before sharing information.

🤔 Did You Know?

Many successful cyber attacks begin with pretexting because attackers spend time researching their victims and creating highly convincing fake identities.
